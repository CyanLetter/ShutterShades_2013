Vernon Courtland Johnson (VCJ)

Model: Ripper
Manufacturer: Powell-Peralta
Year: 1978

The Ripper (bottom row, #7) is arguably the most iconic skateboard graphic in history. Vernon Courtland Johnson (VCJ) created it in 1978 as a follow up to the very successful “Skate and Sword” decks created for Ray “Bones” Rodriguez, the first pro skateboarder hired by Powell-Peralta. VCJ developed the illustration over a period of about six months. In that time, the basic concept of a skull breaking through a barrier went through several different iterations before he settled on the now well-known image.

The “Ripper” set the tone for the Powell-Peralta company and for their graphics. Because the graphic did not have a pro attached to it, it became a generic “Powell” board that sold for many years in various colorways. Over the years Powell developed many related brands that took the skull as their inspiration, such as X-bones (wheels), the Bones Brigade skate team and their winged ripper logo, and Mike McGill’s “Skull & Snake” pro deck (bottom row, #1), thereby solidifying the “Ripper’s” place in skate culture. In 2009, the “Ripper” was the basis for an exhibition called “Rip The Ripper,” in which artists like Shepard Fairey, Lance Mountain and Wes Humpston created their own versions of the Ripper.

Todd Huber of Skatelab, a skatepark and museum located in Simi Valley, CA, suggests that the “Ripper” is the most ripped-off graphic in the skateboard industry and that it has become a symbol of skateboarding and youth culture.
